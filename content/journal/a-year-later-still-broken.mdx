Last summer, WNBA Commissioner Cathy Engelbert stood in front of cameras and promised the league would fix its officiating problem. That was after a season where coaches publicly criticized referees and players openly questioned call consistency. A task force followed. Reports were written. Promises were made.

Nearly two months into this season, the story hasn't changed. Coaches and general managers from eight different teams told reporters that officiating quality remains subpar, and that the league still needs more accountability and investment. One coach put it bluntly: the league needs to pay officials better and go get better talent.

Caitlin Clark has said the same thing publicly — that the league needs to do better, invest in officiating technology, and treat referees like the full-time professionals the job actually requires.

This is the pattern *Unwhistled* documents in detail: a league that reacts to pressure instead of fixing the structure underneath it. A task force isn't a fix. It's a press release. Until the WNBA changes how officiating actually works — not just how it talks about officiating — this conversation repeats every season.
